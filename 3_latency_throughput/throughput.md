# Throughput

**Throughput** is the actual amount of data successfully transferred from one point to another in a given time.

It tells you **how much data really arrives**, not just how much the network _can_ send.

## Throughput Formula

\[
\text{Throughput} = \frac{\text{Total Data Successfully Transferred}}{\text{Total Time Taken}}
\]

**Units of measurement**

bps (bits per second)
Kbps (Kilobits per second)
Mbps (Megabits per second)
Gbps (Gigabits per second)

---

## Simple Meaning

> Throughput = real data speed you actually get

Even if a network is rated at 100 Mbps, the real throughput might be lower (like 70 Mbps) due to delays, congestion, or errors.

---

## Example

- You download a file.
- The network “supports” 100 Mbps.
- But due to congestion and overhead, you only get 60 Mbps.

👉 Your **throughput = 60 Mbps**

---

## Analogy

Think of a water pipe:

- Bandwidth = size of the pipe
- Throughput = actual water flowing through it

If the pipe is blocked or leaky, less water comes out → lower throughput.

---

## Key Point

> Throughput depends on real network conditions like congestion, delays, and packet loss.

---

## Quick Difference

| Term       | Meaning                    |
| ---------- | -------------------------- |
| Bandwidth  | Maximum possible data rate |
| Throughput | Actual achieved data rate  |
